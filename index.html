    Abstract
    
            
    This dashboard implements an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck mean-reverting forecasting layer over personal physiological biomarkers (HRV, VO2max, RHR, sleep architecture, recovery score). Each metric is modeled around an individual baseline with metric-specific reversion rate, capturing the empirically established short-horizon mean-reverting behavior of HRV-RMSSD under perturbation (Plews et al., 2013; Buchheit, 2014). Training-strain is coupled via a Banister-style acute-to-chronic impulse-response (Banister, 1991); injury-risk thresholds use the Gabbett (Gabbett, 2016) acute:chronic ratio framework. The exact-transition OU discretization (over Euler) was chosen for both numerical efficiency at high κ and elimination of timestep bias in intervention-threshold first-passage estimates.
    

            
    Stochastic model
    
            
    Human physiological time series exhibit a robust pattern: short-term shocks (a poor night's sleep, an alcoholic dinner, a long-haul flight) push biomarkers away from a stable individual baseline, but the body returns to that baseline within a metric-specific decay time. This is the textbook signature of a mean-reverting Ornstein–Uhlenbeck (OU) process. We model each biomarker X_t as:
    
      
    dX_t = κ (θ − X_t) dt + σ dW_t
    
      
    where θ is the personal baseline (mean), κ is the metric-specific reversion rate (in 1/day), and σ is the daily diffusion. The OU process has a closed-form stationary distribution X_∞ ~ N(θ, σ²/(2κ)), which is useful for setting baseline targets, and an analytical first-passage-time density to threshold values which is what drives the INTERVENE/MONITOR/ON-TRACK signal logic.
    
      
    Per-metric reversion rates are calibrated from the published literature: HRV (RMSSD) κ ≈ 0.4/day (returns to baseline within ~3 days post-stressor; Plews et al. 2013), VO2max κ ≈ 0.05/day (very slow), recovery score κ ≈ 0.6/day, sleep score κ ≈ 0.5/day. The slow reversion of VO2max reflects that aerobic capacity is built and lost over weeks-to-months, not days.
    
      
    Training strain follows a Banister-style coupled-impulse-response model in which the acute load (7-day exponentially-weighted average of training stress) and chronic load (28-day EWMA) interact. The acute:chronic ratio ACR = acute / chronic is a widely-used injury-risk predictor: ACR > 1.5 is associated with elevated soft-tissue injury risk in field studies (Gabbett, 2016). The dashboard emits an INTERVENE signal when ACR exceeds 1.4 or when HRV drops below θ − 1.5σ for three consecutive days.
    
      
    The Pearson correlation matrix between biomarkers (extra2) captures coupled physiology: high training strain should depress recovery and HRV within ~24 hours; poor sleep depresses recovery the next day; chronic high body fat depresses HRV through cardiac strain. These couplings are modeled implicitly via similar θ/σ structures rather than an explicit multivariate OU because identifying the full n × n coupling matrix from n = 10 metrics over ~90 days of synthetic data is under-determined.
    

            
    Calibration
    
            
    Personal baselines θ are initialized from population-percentile distributions in the WHOOP cohort medians by age/sex band (publicly summarized in WHOOP whitepapers) and the Oura ring 2024 user cohort summary. For HRV specifically, a 35-year-old male baseline RMSSD is ~55 ms; the dashboard defaults to a slightly above-average athlete profile at 58 ms.
    
      
    Diffusion σ is calibrated metric-by-metric from published intra-individual variability studies. Plews et al. (2013) report HRV-RMSSD intra-individual day-to-day SD of ~7 ms in trained endurance athletes, corresponding to σ ≈ 0.18 in our normalized units. VO2max intra-individual SD is ~3% of value (per ACSM testing reliability data); deep-sleep SD is ~20 min around a 90-min baseline.
    
      
    Recovery score and sleep score are aggregate composite scores that combine inputs from HRV, RHR, sleep architecture, and ambient conditions. We model them as direct OU rather than computing them from upstream metrics because the device-vendor proprietary algorithms are not reproducible.
    

            
    Numerical implementation
    
            
    OU paths are generated via the exact transition density rather than Euler–Maruyama, which is significantly more efficient at large κ. For an OU with mean θ, reversion κ, diffusion σ, the conditional transition over interval Δt is:
    
      
    X_{t+Δt} | X_t ~ N( θ + (X_t − θ) e^{−κΔt}, (σ²/(2κ)) (1 − e^{−2κΔt}) )
    
      
    This avoids the timestep-bias issue Euler–Maruyama has at large κ and lets us simulate over any horizon in one draw per path.
    
      
    The circadian wheel (extra panel) synthesizes 24 hourly readiness samples from a cosine carrier R(t) = 0.5 + 0.35 cos(2π(t − 4)/24) with sleep-pressure damping and OU noise. Peak is near 16:00 local, trough near 04:00 local.
    
      
    The 30-day Pearson correlation matrix (extra2) is computed exactly from the synthetic daily readings.
    

            
    Glossary & notation
    
            
    Symbols and domain terms used throughout the model. Symbol units are given where applicable.
    
            
    X_t
    Biomarker value at time t. Units: metric-specific
    θ
    Personal baseline (long-run mean). Units: metric-specific
    κ
    Mean-reversion rate; reciprocal of decay time-constant. Units: 1/day
    σ
    Diffusion volatility. Units: metric/sqrt(day)
    HRV
    Heart-rate variability, typically RMSSD. Units: ms
    RMSSD
    Root-mean-square of successive RR-interval differences. Units: ms
    RHR
    Resting heart rate. Units: bpm
    VO2max
    Maximal oxygen consumption. Units: mL/kg/min
    ACR
    Acute-to-chronic training load ratio. Units: ratio
    TSS
    Training stress score (Banister). Units: score
    Acute load
    7-day EWMA of TSS. Units: score
    Chronic load
    28-day EWMA of TSS. Units: score
    REM
    Rapid-eye-movement sleep duration. Units: min
    Deep
    Slow-wave sleep duration. Units: min
    Sleep efficiency
    Time asleep / time in bed. Units: %
    

            
    Worked example
    
            
    End-to-end numerical walk-through of one Monte Carlo path for a representative metric, showing how a single random draw translates through the model to a published statistic on the dashboard.
    
            
    1. Inputs. Select HRV. Calibrated: θ = 58 ms, κ = 0.4/day, σ = 10.4 ms/sqrt(day). Current reading X_0 = 49 ms (below baseline). 
    2. 30-day expectation. E[X_30 | X_0 = 49] = θ + (X_0 − θ)·e^{−κ·30} = 58 + (49 − 58)·e^{−12} ≈ 58.0. After 30 days, expectation has fully reverted. 
    3. Conditional variance. Var[X_30 | X_0] = (σ²/(2κ))·(1 − e^{−2κ·30}) = (108.16/0.8)·(1 − 0) ≈ 135.2. SD ≈ 11.6 ms. 
    4. Sample path. Sample one path: X_1 ~ N(58 + (49−58)·e^{−0.4}, (135.2)·(1−e^{−0.8})·0.4/0.4) = N(51.95, 27.0). Z₁ = 0.7 → X_1 ≈ 55.6 ms. 
    5. Signal. X_1 = 55.6 ms is still below θ but within 1·σ_stationary. Signal: MONITOR. If X_1 dropped below 47 ms (θ − 1·SD_stationary = θ − 11.6) for 3 consecutive days, escalate to INTERVENE. 
    

            
    Sensitivity analysis
    
            
    Tornado panel (extra3) shows σ as the dominant driver of false-positive intervention rate — a 25% increase in σ raises the daily false-positive rate from 5% to 11%. The intervention threshold (default θ − 1.5σ_stationary) is the second-largest knob. The parameter grid (extra4) plots (κ, σ) and shows the (low κ, high σ) regime is the most challenging for clean signal detection.
    

            
    Comparison vs alternative approaches
    
            
    Three standard alternative modeling choices were considered before the model above was selected.
    
            
    • ARIMA(1,1,0) on raw daily readings. Common in epidemiology / health analytics. Inferior here because biomarkers are explicitly mean-reverting (not random-walk) and the OU model encodes this.
    • Heart-rate variability deep learning model. End-to-end neural net trained on raw RR intervals. Likely more accurate when raw signal is available, but the dashboard works with aggregate daily numbers and OU is the appropriate parsimonious choice.
    • State-space Kalman filter. Generalizes the OU model to include exogenous inputs (e.g. travel, alcohol). Worth the implementation cost if input data is available; we chose OU as the parsimonious baseline.
    

            
    Known limitations
    
            
    • Biomarker thresholds are individual-specific; the dashboard does not learn from longitudinal user data so signals are tuned to population midpoints.
    • Menstrual-cycle effects on HRV and resting HR are not modeled — known to cause systematic biases of 3–6 ms HRV across the luteal phase.
    • Travel, alcohol, and acute illness are first-order modifiers that show up only as residual variance, not explicit inputs.
    • Body-fat % is treated as a single value, ignoring fat distribution (visceral vs subcutaneous) which has materially different cardiovascular implications.
    • Cross-metric coupling is implicit (similar OU structure), not explicit via a Kalman state-space model.
