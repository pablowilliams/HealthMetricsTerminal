    Methodology & model
    
              
    Technical write-up of the stochastic process, calibration approach, and published references for the Metric layer of this dashboard.
    
            
    
            WCAG 2.2 AA · dissertation-grade documentation
          
    

          
    
            
    Stochastic model
    
            
    Each biomarker is modeled as an Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process around a personal baseline θ with a metric-specific mean-reversion rate. This captures the empirical fact that HRV, RHR, and recovery scores deviate transiently around stable individual setpoints rather than diffusing freely:
    
      
    dX_t = κ (θ − X_t) dt + σ dW_t
    
      
    Per-metric κ values: HRV ~0.4/day (returns to baseline within 3 days post-stressor), VO2max ~0.05/day (slow), recovery score ~0.6/day, sleep score ~0.5/day. Training strain follows a coupled process where acute load (7-day EWMA) decays into chronic load (28-day EWMA), and the acute:chronic ratio drives intervention signals.
    

            
    Calibration
    
            
    Personal baselines θ are initialized from population-percentile distributions (WHOOP and Oura cohort medians by age/sex band). Volatility σ is calibrated metric-by-metric from published intra-individual variability studies (e.g. Plews et al. for HRV-RMSSD). The Pearson correlation matrix is computed from 30-day rolling synthetic daily readings.
    

            
    Numerical implementation
    
            
    OU paths are generated via the exact transition density (Gaussian) rather than Euler–Maruyama, which is significantly more efficient at large κ. The circadian wheel synthesizes 24 hourly readiness samples from a cosine carrier with sleep-pressure damping and OU noise. Acute:chronic ratio is computed at each tick.
    

            
    Known limitations
    
            
    • Biomarker thresholds are individual-specific; the dashboard does not learn from longitudinal user data so signals are tuned to population midpoints.
    • Menstrual-cycle effects on HRV and resting HR are not modeled.
    • Travel, alcohol, and acute illness are first-order modifiers that show up only as residual variance, not explicit inputs.
    

            
    References
    
            
    1. Plews, D. J. et al. (2013). Heart Rate Variability in Elite Triathletes. International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance 8(6).
    2. Buchheit, M. (2014). Monitoring training status with HR measures: do all roads lead to Rome? Frontiers in Physiology 5, 73.
    3. Banister, E. W. (1991). Modeling Elite Athletic Performance. In: Physiological Testing of Elite Athletes.
    4. Hjortskov, N. et al. (2004). The effect of mental stress on heart rate variability. European Journal of Applied Physiology 92(1–2).
